Bio-Inspired Frogbot

A four-legged hopping robot modeled on frog locomotion principles.

Overview

As my interest in robotics and autonomous systems continued to grow, I wanted to gain hands-on experience designing moving robots rather than focusing solely on static mechanical systems. For my first robotics project, I set out to build a robot inspired by biological gaits while keeping the mechanical and control systems at a manageable level of complexity.

Design Process

The project began by studying different forms of animal locomotion to determine an appropriate first step into robotics. My goal was to select a design that required meaningful mechanical and programming challenges while remaining achievable with a limited number of actuators and straightforward control logic.

The robot was designed around a laser-cut body and leg structure to minimize fabrication complexity while allowing rapid iteration. Four servo motors were integrated into the body using custom 3D-printed mounting components and servo horns, while additional printed brackets securely mounted the electronics to the top of the robot.

The locomotion sequence was programmed using an Arduino Uno. At the beginning of each movement cycle, the front legs rotate into a stabilizing position to support the robot's body. The rear legs then slowly compress before rapidly extending to generate forward thrust, mimicking the power stroke observed in frog locomotion. This repeating gait provides controlled forward movement while maintaining balance throughout the motion cycle.

CAD & Analysis

The robot was designed entirely in Autodesk Inventor, beginning with individual components for the body and leg assemblies before progressing to a complete digital prototype. Beyond creating the manufacturing models, I assembled all major components including the servo motors, custom servo horns, and structural hardware to verify fit.

A fully constrained assembly was then animated to validate the proposed gait and confirm that the range would produce the intended motion. Creating this proof-of-concept animation allowed potential interference, linkage geometry, and servo positioning to be evaluated digitally before manufacturing, reducing the need for physical redesigns.

Manufacturing & Testing

The chassis and leg components were laser cut to produce lightweight, repeatable parts, while custom servo horns and mounting brackets were manufactured using 3D printing. The electronic components, including the Arduino Uno, servo motors, battery pack, and supporting hardware, were assembled using mechanical fasteners to create a rigid and serviceable platform.

Testing focused on three primary performance metrics: straight-line tracking, balance, and forward speed. Early prototypes successfully walked but moved slower than expected. After researching methods for improving servo performance, I optimized the control code and motion timing, increasing the robot's speed by approximately 0.2 body lengths per second to reach a final speed of 0.4 body lengths per second.

Balance also became a significant design challenge. The battery pack shifted the robot's center of mass forward, reducing stability during the thrust phase of the gait. To correct this, I incorporated counterweights at the rear of the robot, producing a more balanced weight distribution and significantly improving straight-line motion and overall stability during motion.

These iterative improvements resulted in a robot that consistently exceeded the original design objectives.

Results & Lessons

The completed robot exceeded the initial design objectives. The original performance targets were to maintain straight-line motion, remain stable throughout operation, and achieve a minimum speed of 0.25 body lengths per second.

During testing, the robot consistently maintained linear motion while reaching approximately 0.4 body lengths per second, doubling the original performance goal. The project also provided valuable experience integrating mechanical design, embedded programming, and electromechanical assembly into a single system.

More importantly, this project served as a introduction to robotics and established a foundation for more advanced future work.
