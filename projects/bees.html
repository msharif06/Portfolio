Bee Monitoring Chamber

Enclosure designed to host 2 controlled & monitorable bee experiments.

Overview

Designed a modular research enclosure to support controlled inoculation experiments on bumblebees while enabling continuous observation of bee health and behavior. The system combines custom CAD, 3D-printed components, acrylic construction, automated imaging, and removable feeding mechanisms to create a repeatable experimental environment that minimizes contamination while supporting long-term biological studies.

Requirements

    The enclosure was designed to satisfy several biological and engineering constraints:

    -------------------------------------------------------------

  • House two independent artificial bumblebee hives within a single enclosure.
  • Replicate the dark, enclosed conditions of natural ground nests.
  • Allow safe feeding without compromising containment.
  • Enable easy sanitation and maintenance between experimental trials.
  • Prevent bee escape while allowing unrestricted feeding behavior.

Design Process

The enclosure was developed through multiple design iterations to balance biological requirements with manufacturability and ease of use. Because bumblebees naturally nest underground, the enclosure was designed to maintain a dark interior while providing sufficient access for observation and maintenance.

Special attention was given to feeding, camera integration, and contamination control. Custom artificial hives were developed alongside removable feeding systems and modular access ports, allowing researchers to service the enclosure without disrupting the experimental environment.

CAD & Analysis

The enclosure and artificial hives were designed entirely in CAD using a modular architecture for rapid prototyping and future modifications.

Each hive incorporates a slitted feeding floor positioned above a retractable nutrient tray. This geometry allows bees to reach nectar through the openings using their proboscis while preventing escape or contamination. The enclosure also integrates dedicated mounting ports for a Raspberry Pi and infrared camera, enabling continuous monitoring without introducing external light into the hive.

The modular design allows individual components to be replaced, reprinted, or upgraded independently as experimental requirements evolve.

Manufacturing & Testing

The enclosure was fabricated using laser-cut acrylic panels combined with custom 3D-printed structural and hive components.

Prototype testing focused on verifying feeding accessibility, containment reliability, camera placement, maintenance procedures, and overall usability during laboratory operation. The removable feeding trays simplified nutrient replacement and cleaning, while the modular construction reduced assembly time and supported rapid design revisions throughout development.

Results & Lessons

The completed enclosure provides a controlled experimental environment capable of supporting microbiome inoculation studies while enabling continuous behavioral observation.

By integrating modular CAD design, additive manufacturing, embedded imaging hardware, and biologically informed design principles, the project demonstrates how engineering solutions can improve repeatability and data collection in biological research. The design also establishes a flexible platform that can be adapted for future experiments involving different sensors, environmental controls, or hive configurations.

This project strengthened my experience in CAD, rapid prototyping, design for research, embedded hardware integration, laboratory equipment design, and multidisciplinary engineering collaboration.
